How Anxiety Affects Mental and Physical Health

By Michael Hetherington · October 10, 2024

Anxiety is often described as a "mental health" condition — as if it lives only in the mind. But anyone who has experienced significant anxiety knows this is only half the picture. Anxiety is a whole-body experience. It affects cognition, mood, digestion, immunity, sleep, hormonal balance, and cardiovascular function. Understanding how anxiety moves through the entire system is the first step toward addressing it at a level that actually produces change.

The Mental and Emotional Effects of Anxiety

Cognitive effects: Chronic anxiety impairs executive function — the prefrontal cortex's capacity for clear thinking, decision-making, planning, and flexible problem-solving. Under sustained anxiety, the brain allocates resources toward threat-detection at the expense of higher reasoning. This is why anxious people often feel foggy, indecisive, and unable to concentrate — the brain is literally running a different program.

Memory is also affected. Chronic cortisol elevation — the hallmark of sustained anxiety — is neurotoxic to the hippocampus, the brain region responsible for forming and retrieving memories. Anxiety can both impair the formation of new memories and distort the retrieval of old ones, particularly in relation to threatening content.

Emotional effects: Anxiety and depression frequently co-occur — not as separate conditions, but as different expressions of the same underlying nervous system state. Anxiety can manifest as irritability, emotional reactivity, difficulty tolerating uncertainty, and a pervasive sense of dread or unease that doesn't attach to any specific trigger.

Social effects: Anxiety commonly drives social avoidance. When social situations are experienced as threatening — whether through fear of judgement, conflict, or performance anxiety — the instinct is to withdraw. Over time, this avoidance narrows the person's world and deepens the sense of isolation.

The Physical Effects of Anxiety

Cardiovascular system: Anxiety activates the sympathetic nervous system, elevating heart rate and blood pressure. In the short term, this is an adaptation. In the long term, chronic sympathetic activation contributes to hypertension, increased risk of cardiac arrhythmia, and elevated cardiovascular disease risk.

Digestive system: The gut-brain axis means the digestive system is exquisitely sensitive to nervous system state. Anxiety commonly produces IBS symptoms, nausea, appetite changes, altered gut motility, and increased intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"). People with chronic anxiety frequently report digestive symptoms as a prominent feature — and digestive healing often stalls until the anxiety is addressed.

Immune system: Chronic cortisol elevation suppresses immune function. This is why chronically anxious people tend to get sick more frequently, take longer to recover, and show elevated markers of systemic inflammation. There is a measurable correlation between chronic anxiety, inflammatory conditions, and accelerated ageing at the cellular level.

Sleep: Anxiety is one of the most common causes of insomnia — both difficulty falling asleep (an overactivated mind and nervous system) and difficulty staying asleep (particularly the 1–3am window of waking, which Chinese medicine associates with the Liver). Poor sleep then worsens anxiety, creating a self-reinforcing cycle that is difficult to break without addressing the nervous system directly.

Hormonal effects: Prolonged HPA-axis activation from chronic anxiety disrupts the production and regulation of multiple hormones — including thyroid hormones, reproductive hormones, and the DHEA-cortisol ratio. This can manifest as fatigue, hormonal irregularities, reduced libido, and what is sometimes called "adrenal fatigue" — a state of nervous system and hormonal exhaustion from sustained activation.

The Chinese Medicine View of Anxiety

Traditional Chinese medicine does not separate mental and physical health — they are inseparable expressions of the same energy system. In Chinese medicine, anxiety is primarily associated with two organ systems: the Heart and the Liver.

The Heart governs the Shen — the mind-spirit — and is responsible for the clarity, presence, and peace of the mind. When Heart energy is disturbed, the Shen becomes unsettled, manifesting as anxiety, racing thoughts, insomnia, and difficulty finding calm. The Liver, meanwhile, governs the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. When Liver Qi stagnates — through suppressed emotion, unresolved conflict, or chronic stress — the blocked energy generates heat that rises upward and disturbs the Heart Shen. This is the Chinese medicine explanation of why emotional suppression and unresolved stress so reliably produce anxiety.
