Tapping Away Anxiety with EFT Tapping

By Michael Hetherington · July 22, 2024

Anxiety is not a thought problem. It is a nervous system state.

If it were simply a thought problem, you could reason your way out of it. You could think "there is no real danger here" and your body would relax. But anyone who has experienced anxiety knows that's not how it works. The body keeps responding as if there is danger — even when you know cognitively that you're safe.

This is why EFT Tapping works so well for anxiety, and why approaches that only target thoughts — without targeting the body — often provide only partial relief.

What Anxiety Actually Is

Anxiety is the body's threat-response system activating without the presence of real, immediate danger. The amygdala — the brain's threat-detection centre — fires an alarm signal. The nervous system shifts into sympathetic activation: heart rate elevates, breathing becomes shallow, muscles tense, digestion slows, and attention narrows to scan for the source of the threat.

In the presence of a real threat, this is lifesaving. But in chronic anxiety, this activation happens in response to memories, anticipations, social situations, or internal states — not real, present-moment danger. And crucially, once the amygdala learns to respond to something as threatening, it continues doing so automatically — below the level of conscious thought.

This is why telling yourself "there's nothing to worry about" doesn't work. The amygdala doesn't take instructions from the prefrontal cortex. It responds to direct signals — and EFT appears to deliver exactly that.

How EFT Tapping Calms Anxiety

Research by Dr Dawson Church and colleagues has shown that tapping on acupressure points sends a direct calming signal to the amygdala via the vagus nerve and the body's stress response pathways. Tapping appears to down-regulate amygdala reactivity while simultaneously holding the anxiety-producing thought in awareness.

This combination is the key. By thinking about the source of anxiety (which activates the amygdala) while simultaneously tapping (which sends a calming signal), the brain begins to dissociate the triggering stimulus from the fear response. Over repeated sessions, the stimulus stops producing the anxiety response — the same process that underlies Exposure Therapy, but with the additional physiological calming that acupressure appears to provide.

In practice, this means you can feel the anxiety reducing — sometimes within minutes — as you tap through the points.

A Simple EFT Sequence for Anxiety

Step 1: Rate your anxiety from 0–10. Notice where you feel it in your body — chest tightness, stomach knots, racing heart, shallow breath, restless energy.

Setup statement (tap karate chop point, repeat three times):

  • "Even though I feel this anxiety in my [chest / stomach / whole body], I deeply and completely accept myself."
  • "Even though my body is responding as if something is wrong, I choose to let my nervous system settle."
  • "Even though I feel this anxious energy, I am safe right now and I accept myself."

Round 1 — Tune into the anxiety: (tap through eyebrow, side of eye, under eye, under nose, chin, collarbone, under arm, top of head)

  • "This anxiety."
  • "This feeling in my [chest / stomach / body]."
  • "My body is in alarm mode."
  • "I can feel this activation."
  • "My nervous system is on high alert."
  • "All this anxious energy."
  • "I'm just acknowledging it."
  • "This is what anxiety feels like in my body."

Round 2 — Send a safety signal:

  • "I am safe right now."
  • "There is no immediate danger."
  • "I give my nervous system permission to settle."
  • "My body can begin to release this tension."
  • "I am allowed to feel calm."
  • "My breath is slowing."
  • "I am grounded in this moment."
  • "I choose peace over alarm."

Step 2: Take a slow breath. Re-rate the anxiety from 0–10. Repeat the sequence, updating your words to match the current sensation, until the rating drops to 2 or below.

Treating Anxiety at the Root

The sequence above is for acute anxiety relief — great for moments of overwhelm, panic, or nervous activation. But for chronic anxiety, the real work is at the root: identifying and clearing the specific memories, beliefs, and patterns that are keeping the amygdala primed for alarm.

Typical root-level anxiety sources include: a childhood environment that was unpredictable or unsafe; specific traumatic experiences where the body learned that the world is dangerous; long-standing beliefs about one's inadequacy or vulnerability; and chronic stress that has kept the nervous system in activation for so long it has become the new normal.
