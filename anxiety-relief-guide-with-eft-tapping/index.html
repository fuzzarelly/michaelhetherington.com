Understanding anxiety through a Chinese medicine lens

In Western medicine, anxiety is primarily seen as a neurological or psychological condition — managed through medication, cognitive behavioural therapy, or a combination of both. These approaches have value, but they often miss a crucial dimension: the role of the body.

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) has understood for centuries that emotions are not just mental events — they are stored in specific organ systems, influence the flow of Qi and blood, and, when unresolved, create both psychological and physical symptoms over time.

The TCM organs most associated with anxiety

The Heart in Chinese medicine is the organ most associated with anxiety, mental restlessness, and insomnia. When Heart Qi is deficient or disturbed, symptoms include palpitations, panic attacks, an inability to quiet the mind, and difficulty sleeping.

The Kidneys govern the body's deep reserves and are associated with fear at the emotional level. Kidney deficiency can manifest as existential anxiety, fear of the future, and a pervasive sense of insecurity — even when the circumstances don't justify it.

The Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi through the body. When Liver Qi stagnates (often from chronic stress, frustration, or emotional suppression), it creates tension, irritability, and the kind of anxious overthinking that loops without resolution.

What EFT Tapping does that other approaches don't

EFT (Emotional Freedom Techniques) combines acupressure tapping on specific meridian points with cognitive processing. When you tap while focusing on a fear, memory, or stressor, several things happen simultaneously:

  • The amygdala (the brain's threat-detection centre) receives a calming signal from the tapping
  • The body's fight-or-flight response begins to deactivate
  • The cognitive brain comes back online — enabling rational processing
  • The emotional charge attached to the specific memory or fear begins to dissolve

This is why EFT can produce results that talk therapy alone often can't. You are not just thinking about the issue differently — you are clearing the stored charge in the nervous system that was keeping the anxiety pattern in place.

Practical EFT for anxiety relief

Urgent EFT — for immediate stress and anxiety

When anxiety spikes, a 5–10 minute EFT session can quickly calm the nervous system. Tap through the basic sequence (eyebrow, side of eye, under eye, under nose, chin, collarbone, under arm, top of head) while describing exactly what you are feeling — the physical sensations, the thoughts, the intensity. Don't try to fix or reframe anything. Just describe it accurately and tap.

Therapeutic EFT — for lasting change

Deeper therapeutic work requires longer sessions — 15–20+ minutes — and focuses on specific memories, core beliefs, and the original events that created the anxiety pattern. This is the level where EFT creates lasting change rather than temporary relief.

For best results, aim for 15–20 minutes of focused EFT daily for at least 30 days when beginning.

Diet, lifestyle, and Chinese medicine for anxiety

Foods that support the nervous system

In Chinese medicine, warm, cooked foods are generally preferable to cold and raw — particularly for people with anxiety, as cold foods are considered energetically cooling and can weaken digestive Qi, which in turn affects the Heart and Liver.

Foods that support the nervous system according to TCM include: bone broths, dark leafy greens, walnuts (particularly good for Kidney energy), jujube dates (traditional Heart tonic), and oats.

Sleep as a foundation

The Liver is most active between 1–3am according to Chinese medicine. If you consistently wake during this window, it is often a sign of Liver Qi stagnation — and working with EFT to clear frustration, resentment, and suppressed emotion can significantly improve sleep quality.

Exercise and Qi Gong

Moderate aerobic exercise is beneficial for moving Liver Qi. However, excessive vigorous exercise can deplete Kidney energy over time, particularly if you are already running on low reserves. Qi Gong and Tai Chi offer an alternative that simultaneously cultivates and circulates Qi without depleting it.
